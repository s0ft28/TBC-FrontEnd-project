Predator Hunting

Master the pursuit of coyotes, foxes, bobcats, and other predators.

What is Predator Hunting?

Predator hunting involves pursuing carnivorous animals like coyotes, foxes, and bobcats. It's often done for wildlife management, fur, or simply the challenge. Predator hunting is unique because hunters typically call animals TO them rather than pursuing the animals.

This style of hunting has grown rapidly in popularity due to year-round or extended seasons, minimal gear requirements, and the intense excitement when a predator responds to a call and comes charging in.

Common Predator Species

Coyote

Most popular predator, found nationwide

Red Fox

Intelligent and beautiful, prized fur

Gray Fox

Tree-climbing fox of wooded areas

Bobcat

Elusive feline, challenging to call

Raccoon

Often hunted at night with dogs

Mountain Lion

Apex predator, limited hunting areas

Essential Gear

  • Rifle: Flat-shooting calibers like .223, .22-250, or .243. AR-15 platform is popular for quick follow-up shots.
  • Electronic Caller: Plays distress sounds and predator vocalizations. FoxPro and FOXPRO are popular brands.
  • Decoy: Motion decoys that mimic injured prey attract visual predators.
  • Camouflage: Full camo including face mask and gloves. Match the terrain.
  • Shooting Sticks: Bipod or tripod for steady shots on small targets.

Hunting Techniques

Stand Calling

Set up downwind, use calls to bring predators in. Typical stands last 15-30 minutes.

Prey Distress

Rabbit, bird, or rodent distress calls trigger the predator's hunting instinct.

Challenge Howling

Coyote howls and barks provoke territorial responses, especially during breeding season.

Night Hunting

Where legal, hunting at night with lights or thermal optics is highly effective.

Calling Sequences

Successful predator calling often follows a pattern:

  1. Start quiet - Begin with low-volume calls for nearby predators
  2. Increase volume - Gradually increase to reach distant animals
  3. Vary sounds - Switch between prey types (rabbit, bird, rodent)
  4. Add vocalizations - Mix in coyote yips or pup distress
  5. Stay patient - Continue calling for 20-30 minutes per stand

Quick Facts

  • Season Fall through winter (species dependent)
  • Best Locations Prairies, CRP fields, forest edges
  • License Required State small game license + stamps

Beginner Tips

  • • Start on public land or a preserve
  • • Hunt early morning or late afternoon
  • • Focus on cover edges and food sources
  • • Keep your gun ready - flushes are fast!
  • • Walk slowly and pause frequently
